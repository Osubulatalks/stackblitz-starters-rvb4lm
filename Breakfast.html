Morning Breakfast

The most meaningless meal of the day.

Eggs and bacon for morning breakfast were aggressively marketed by propagandist Edward Bernays on behalf of the food industry.

There's nothing healthy or nutritious about a morning meal. In medieval Europe, eating early in the day was only a necessity for those who worked so early that they had to eat early, or for the elderly and the sick.

Thomas Aquinas a 13th-century theologian believed that it was a sin to eat too early in the day. In the Roman religious tradition, eating before morning mass was unacceptable because fasting was a spiritual obligation and therefore breakfast literally meant breaking the fast.

In Africa, until the mid-21st century, our grandparents never ate the morning meal. I remember in the mid-80s and until the mid-90s, my grandparents woke up early in the morning, drank a tumbler of water and left for the farm.

They would tender the crops while keeping a keen eye on the cows. Then they would leave the farm at 1 o'clock where my grandmother would go home to prepare lunch as my grandfather drove the animals to the river to drink water. They would then meet at 2 o'clock for their first meal of the day.

Morning breakfast is an alien dietary habit in the African culture.

Similarly, as pupils in rural neighbourhoods, we never ate the morning meal. We just woke up, prepared ourselves and left for school while running lest we be punished by our teachers for lateness. At 1 o'clock we would trek back home to eat our first meal of the day. Suffice it to say, we were jovial, energetic, lean and healthy.

As people increasingly migrated from rural areas to urban areas to work for an employer, rather than working for themselves on their own land, they lost control of their time and had to work long, uninterrupted days without sustenance. They would therefore eat a huge morning meal that allowed them to work longer hours.

This Industrial Revolution and the move from farms to factories normalized the idea of a heavy morning meal before going to work or school. "Breakfast is the most important meal of the day" was a marketing strategy created by Nestle, Kellogg and General mills to market their cereals eg Weetabix, Oats and bread.

The Circadian Clock

The Circadian clock or rhythm is a 24-hour internal clock in our brain that regulates cycles of alertness and sleepiness by responding to light changes in our environment.

The circadian clock controls our sleep/wake cycle. It also regulates metabolism, immunity, fertility and other biological functions.

Our physiology and behaviour are regulated by the Earth’s rotation around its axis. This biological circadian system has evolved to help mammals adapt to changes in the environment and anticipate changes in radiation, temperature, and food availability.

One of the adaptations is the change in the internal activities at dawn just before sunrise. During this time the cock crows, the donkey brays and the birds chirping. Do you think these mammals have a timekeeper? Yes, the timekeeper is the circadian clock.

Human beings have these changes too.

At 4 AM in the morning, the human body releases a thread of hormones known as the counter-regulatory hormones.

These hormones are

  1. Glucagon
  2. Adrenaline
  3. Cortisol
  4. Growth hormone

These hormones raise blood sugar in the morning. They also raise blood pressure and pulse rate. The rising blood glucose level prepares you to face the day.
Growth hormone prepares your muscles for the day while adrenaline wakes up your mind to face the morning.

The body has several regulatory mechanisms to replenish energy when needed.

This rising blood glucose will remain till a few minutes after midday when it begins dropping. If it falls, the body will now use another mechanism to mobilize more glucose from muscle tissues and the liver, and this includes converting fats in the liver into glucose. This mechanism is known as Gluconeogenesis.

Therefore you don't have to eat anything in the morning because the body has already availed enough resources through the action of counter-regulatory hormones.

"But, we need energy..."

Energy does not come from the stomach. Energy does not come from the food you have just eaten. Energy comes from a complex process in the mitochondria known as the KREB CYCLE. The entire process begins in the liver or skeletal muscles, not the stomach.

THE KREBS CYCLE

THE KREB CYCLE is a series of biochemical processes that happen inside the mitochondria. Fats, carbs and proteins are metabolized from the fat stores in the liver or muscles to release a special molecule known as ACETYL-COA. This molecule is fed into the KREB Cycle.

This molecule undergoes several biochemical steps inside the mitochondria to release a certain reducing agent called NADH.

This NADH is fed in a respiratory channel inside the mitochondria in a process called oxidative phosphorylation where huge amounts of energy are produced in the form of a compound known as ATP. Energy comes from stored glucose known as glycogen in the liver & muscle tissues and stored fatty acids in the adipose tissues.

The Kreb cycle happens in the mitochondria. It's purpose is to release fuel for energy use.

Therefore if there is low blood glucose and yet the body needs energy, the body can turn proteins into glucose in a process known as gluconeogenesis while fats are converted into fuel called ketones in a process called Ketosis/ketogenesis.

For a human body to get energy, it must harness acetyl-CoA from glucose or ketones. It is therefore fallacious to think that energy comes from the food you have just eaten. The food you have just eaten must undergo digestion and absorption then storage before the body breaks the stored food into Acetyl-CoA.
